Neon Genesis Evangelion: 10 Undeniable Ways That It Changed Mecha Anime Forever

By  Theo Kogod

There are many reasons why Neon Genesis Evangelion is a beloved favorite series among anime fans. Decades after it was originally released, it still has new merchandise being made and even received a remake that tapped into that fan nostalgia while reaching a whole new generation of viewers.

This series fundamentally changed what a mecha anime could do. Before its release, most mecha series were little more than loose plots used to justify action scenes involving oversized military hardware. Evangelion defied expectations as it explored philosophical and religious concepts with a cast of rich complex characters. Here are ten ways the series changes mecha anime forever.

10 Strong Women

In the modern world, sexism remains entrenched in society, but at least anime and other forms of media seem to be getting better at showcasing strong women in leading roles. For a long time, women--especially young women--were shown in mecha anime as little more than romance interests for the hero, femme Fatales, comic relief, or sexual objects.

Remove Ads

Three of the four main characters of Evangelion are women. Two of them, Rei Ayanami and Asuka Langley, are young women still in school who pilot the EVA mech suits used to fight angels. The third, Misato Katsuragi, is a commander at NERV as well as the guardian of both Asuka and the final protagonist Shinji Ikari. All of the women are complex characters with their own agency, and while some fit into classic tropes, none exist just for the male gaze.

9 Anti-Heroic Male Protagonist

The protagonist of Neon Genesis Evangelion is Shinji Ikari, a teenager plagued by depression, anxiety, abandonment issues, and self-doubt. At the cusp of manhood, he is crippled by childhood fears and traumas, worsened by the dismissive way his father treats him (when deigning to speak to Shinji at all).

Remove Ads

To have such a character was a groundbreaking bit of storytelling at the time. While many fans complain that Shinji is whiny and cowardly, he's a perfect antihero, contrasting the usual brave militant archetypal protagonists of mecha anime. Shinji doesn't want to be the best or defeat his enemies. He just wants to be loved and to stop feeling afraid.

8 Philosophy

While Evangelion is far from the first mecha anime to discuss philosophy, but while most previous mecha series dealt with dime-store navel-gazing that explored concepts of the hero's personal individualism the merits of peace versus nonviolence. Evangelion took a deep dive into its approach to exploring the meaning of things.

The show's turning point was the episode "In the Still Darkness," in which the power went out at the NERV base of operations when an angel attacked. The three children who piloted the EVAs pondered the nature of angels and their plight with man. That night, as they sat beneath the stars, watching the city lights slowly turn back on, Rei offered up her insight: "Man fears the darkness and so he scrapes away at its edges with fire." From there, the thinking only got deeper and more complex.

7 Religion

Religion is one of those complex personal topics that can make or break a work of art. As Japan is a country whose population mostly consists of Buddhists and which was historically rooted in Shinto, these are the two faiths most commonly depicted in anime. Mecha anime is a genre that exists to justify fights between giant robots, making it particularly unequipped to deal with profound spiritual matters.

Remove Ads

Despite that, Evangelion is rooted in Christianity. Its story inspired (very, very loosely) by the Dead Sea Scrolls, which are referenced throughout the show. Christian symbolism permeates every episode. Biblical figures like Adam and Lilith appear while crucifixion and the Lance of Longinus are also heavily featured. This was unheard of for mecha anime at the time.

6 Gnosticism

Gnosticism is a branch of mysticism that was incredibly popular among early Christians, though its roots predate Christianity and were part of Jewish mystical teachings long after the spread of the newer religion. Gnosticism is concerned with the pursuit of spiritual knowledge and has several central characters and themes which set it apart from mainstream Biblical teachings.

Remove Ads

NERV's founders and ruling council are dedicated to understanding the angels and changing humanity to align with the potential locked away within the human soul, incredibly gnostic concepts. In fact, there are so many gnostic elements at work in Evangelion, the topic could fill several books and still not address them all.

5 Symbolism

Evangelion is filled with symbolism. There's religious symbolism, Freudian symbolism, Jungian symbolism, and even technological symbolism. Some of the symbolism is pretty obscure or difficult to recognize on the first watch through the show. Other elements, like seeing the sky filled with crosses as multiple crucified angels explode in the sky over Earth, are pretty hard to miss (if perhaps a bit messy and convoluted).

Remove Ads

Not all of the symbolism in Evangelion is equally smart or even logically coherent. However, the abundance of so many meaningful symbols gave the work a literary significance that raised the stakes for other mecha anime to have to follow.

4 Dream Sequences

In the science-heavy world of mecha anime, space travel and rocket propulsion and supercomputers are common. After all, mecha are about science. And skyscraper-high robots making things explode.

Evangelion is a bit more abstract than all that. While most of the series is incredibly grounded in character-driven drama and the series offers scientific explanations for many of the magical elements, there are numerous points when the plot goes off into prolonged dream sequences. In fact, the last two episodes of the main series comprise one extended dream sequence with art-house theatre effects animated in as the show leans hard into surrealism.

3 Hard Science Exploring Fantasy Elements

Neon Genesis Evangelion is a story about teenagers piloting giant biomechanical robotic angel clones as weapons to fight with actual angels for the survival (or engineered extinction) of humanity. Any story involving angels is bound to have some magical elements to it, but this is a unique series because science is just as important as the magical concepts like angels and the soul.

Many of the science concepts presented in the show are real, though perhaps calling all of them "hard science" is a bit of a stretch. However, there is a logic behind the computer systems, bioengineering, and mechanization which is still practically unheard of in most mecha science fantasy stories.

2 Psychology

In the long history of their portrayal throughout anime, mech pilots tend to repeatedly be portrayed as either impishly playful or being the sorts of people who are just as hard and unyielding as the metal their machines are made from. The three children who pilot the EVAs in Evangelion break this mold by being psychologically complex, damaged people.

All three suffer because of issues with their parents -- a deeply Freudian concept. Fears about social acceptance and rejection, abandonment, loss of a loved one, and the nature of happiness echo through the show and are fully explored in the final two-episode psychoanalytic climax that feels like a thesis on the works of Freud and Lacan.

1 Loneliness

Remove Ads

Humans are social creatures. When denied social interaction, loneliness sets in, whether it is caused by the loneliness of space or just a general sense of isolation. Conversely, one can feel lonely when in a crowd, lost in the multitude of people.

Most mecha anime characters never seem to feel lonely. They may be utterly alone, but they still remain confident and self-assured, or when they have doubts, these never have anything to do with isolation. Addressing boneless creates a sense of realism that makes Evangelion a more nuanced and much more personal narrative than other mecha anime.
